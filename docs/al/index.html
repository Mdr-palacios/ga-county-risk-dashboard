Quick context HB 56 (2011) and the 2025 SB 53/63/158/55 package: why every AL county is in the model

Alabama's HB 56 (the Beason-Hammon Act, 2011) was one of the harshest state immigration laws ever passed. Federal courts struck down most of it (school checks, contract bars, harboring), but the core 'show me your papers' provision survived as Ala. Code § 31-13-5: every state and local law enforcement officer must attempt to determine the immigration status of anyone they reasonably suspect of being unlawfully present during any lawful stop, detention, or arrest. That mandate is still in force in every AL county in 2026.

In 2025, Gov. Kay Ivey signed a four-bill package that layers a hard anti-sanctuary regime on top of HB 56. SB 53 makes it a Class C felony for any state or local official to adopt or enforce a sanctuary policy, and conditions all state funding on documented ICE cooperation. SB 63 requires every law enforcement agency to honor ICE detainers and to participate in 287(g) when offered. SB 158 mandates E-Verify for all public and private employers and authorizes the Attorney General to investigate violations. SB 55 criminalizes harboring or transporting an undocumented person as a separate state offense.

Practically, that means every one of Alabama's 67 counties is a forced cooperator with ICE in 2026, regardless of local politics. In this risk model, the HB 56 / SB 53 baseline contributes a small statewide floor (5 points). The reason a county lands in Critical or High tier is what gets layered on top: 287(g) agreements, ICE detention infrastructure, Hispanic concentration in poultry-processing counties, and total population. The legislative package is the floor; the tier system shows where the ceiling is highest.

Enforcement infrastructure The 287(g) explosion: 1 agency to 40+ in twelve months

287(g) is the federal program that deputizes state and local police to perform some immigration enforcement functions. As of June 2026, ICE's participating agencies file lists 47 agency-level Alabama agreements, covering roughly 40 distinct sheriff's offices, police departments, and state agencies; up from just one Alabama agency in early 2025. The dominant model is Task Force Model (TFM), which authorizes officers to question and arrest people about immigration status during routine encounters: traffic stops, calls for service, and street investigations. A smaller number of jails use Jail Enforcement Model (JEM) and Warrant Service Officer (WSO).

The expansion is geographically concentrated. Baldwin County (Gulf Coast) signed five separate agreements in 2025 alone. Cherokee County (northeast) has four. The North Alabama poultry belt (DeKalb, Marshall, Franklin, Blount, Cullman, Limestone) shows the next densest cluster, exactly where Latino workforce concentration is highest. SB 63 (2025) requires every AL law enforcement agency to participate when offered, so the trajectory is one direction.

In this risk model, 287(g) contributes up to 30 points by type: TFM is weighted heaviest (22 points), JEM and WSO add smaller amounts, and counties with four or more agency agreements receive a saturation bonus. Counties currently in Critical tier are not Critical because of HB 56; they are Critical because TFM-deputized officers now operate during routine traffic enforcement.

Detention infrastructure Etowah reopens, Pickens expands, Baldwin opens: AL's detention footprint in 2026

In September 2025, ICE reopened the Etowah County Detention Center in Gadsden, three years after it had been closed following a long campaign documenting medical neglect, suicide attempts, and hunger strikes by detained men. The new contract authorizes up to 865 beds and is one of the largest single ICE bed-expansions in the South in 2025-2026. Etowah's reopening was paired with the opening of a new Baldwin County facility (2025) and the expansion of the Pickens County Detention Center in Carrollton, which has long held ICE detainees under a county jail contract.

Detention proximity matters because it shapes who an ICE field office can hold and for how long. A 287(g) arrest in a county adjacent to detention is a transfer of hours, not days. The further a detainee is moved from their family, the harder visitation, bond posting, and attorney access become; and the more pressure detainees feel to sign voluntary removal orders.

In this model, host counties (Etowah, Pickens, Baldwin) carry the full 25-point detention factor; counties adjacent to a host carry 10 points. The detention map is changing fast: rumors of additional contracts in Mobile and Madison have circulated, and this score will be updated as ICE publishes new facility lists.
