Quick context What is HB 538, and why does it matter for this dashboard?

HB 538 is Mississippi's 2026 anti-sanctuary law. Passed by the Senate in March 2026 and headed to Gov. Tate Reeves, it requires every state agency, county, municipality, college, and law-enforcement body to cooperate with ICE, share information on "all aliens within the state," and honor immigration detainer requests. The MS Attorney General has authority to investigate and prosecute any entity that refuses.

Practically, that means every one of the 82 counties is a forced cooperator with ICE, regardless of local politics. Sanctuary policies are no longer an option under state law. HB 538 builds on a separate criminalization push (SB 2114, HB 1037) that turns immigration violations into state crimes and explicitly funds 287(g) cooperation.

In this risk model, HB 538 contributes a baseline score that applies statewide. The reason a county lands in Critical or High tier is not HB 538 alone, but what gets layered on top: 287(g) agreements, the 2019 raid footprint that still shapes who lives in fear of ICE, ICE detention infrastructure (Adams County Correctional in Natchez holds ~2,154 detainees a day), and meatpacking employer density. HB 538 is the floor; the tier system shows where the ceiling is highest.

Surveillance infrastructure Flock Safety ALPRs: 891 cameras feeding the same network that runs background checks

Flock Safety automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are stationary, solar-powered cameras that capture every passing vehicle's plate, make, model, color, and unique features, then route that data to a searchable national network shared across thousands of police agencies. DeFlock.me has mapped roughly 891 Flock cameras across 41 Mississippi cities and towns, with the heaviest density in Madison County (Ridgeland alone has ~55 cameras), DeSoto County (Olive Branch and Southaven), Harrison County (Gulfport and D'Iberville), and Hinds County (Jackson).

Two things make this directly relevant to immigration enforcement. First, the Electronic Frontier Foundation documented in May 2026 that law enforcement agencies in Mississippi, Missouri, Texas, and Illinois are using Flock to run background checks, the kind of dragnet use Flock claims it does not permit. Second, EFF has separately shown Flock being used to surveil protesters and activists, and Vicksburg just approved 10 more cameras on April 6, 2026, partially grant-funded. The cumulative effect is a state-spanning mesh that ICE and 287(g) sheriffs can query, often through informal channels and shared logins, without ever filing a public records request.

In this risk model, Flock contributes up to 15 points, scaled by camera density: 15 for heavy mesh (40+ cameras in the county, like Madison or DeSoto), 12 for dense (20-39), 9 for moderate (10-19), 6 for light (5-9), and 3 for any confirmed Flock city. Crossing a county line with a Flock mesh is not a neutral event for an undocumented worker or organizer; it is a recorded vehicle movement that can be retrieved months later.

Why 2019 still matters The August 7, 2019 ICE raids: 680 arrests in one day

On August 7, 2019, ICE executed coordinated workplace raids at seven poultry processing plants across six Mississippi cities: Morton (Koch Foods and PH Food, Scott County), Carthage (Pearl River Foods, Leake), Canton (PECO Foods, Madison), Pelahatchie (A&B Inc., Rankin), Walnut Grove (PECO Foods, Leake), Bay Springs (PECO Foods, Jasper), and Sebastopol (PECO Foods, Scott). Roughly 680 workers were arrested in a single day.

The aftermath shaped the politics of immigrant labor in Mississippi for years. Families in Morton, Forest, Laurel, and Carthage were broken up overnight. Many workers were released on humanitarian grounds with ankle monitors and remain under ICE supervision today. Federal indictments hit personnel at two smaller companies (Pearl River Foods and A&B), while executives at Koch Foods and PECO Foods, who had publicly endorsed Gov. Phil Bryant and given thousands to Mississippi Republicans, were never charged. The asymmetry was the lesson.

In this model, the five counties where plants were directly hit (Scott, Leake, Madison, Rankin, Jasper) carry a 25-point raid factor. Adjacent counties where significant numbers of affected families lived (Jones, Forrest, Newton, Smith, Neshoba, Simpson) carry 12 points. This is not historical context; it is current threat geography.
